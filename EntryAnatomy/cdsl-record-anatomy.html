The SLP1 ASCII transliteration (non-trivial mappings)
SLP1AIUfFxEOMHNYwqRTDSzG
IASTāīūṛṝḷaiauṃḥṅñṭḍṇthdhśṣgh
<L> record number — the stable unique id of the record in the Cologne digitization (not in the print)
<pc> print locus: volume–column for PWG (7-1655), page,column for MW — the bridge back to the scan
<k1> = key1, the normalized lookup key in SLP1: no accents, no compound marks — use it for matching, dedup, joins
<k2> = key2, closer to the printed form: keeps the udātta accent (hema/n) and, in MW, compound dashes (hema—kakza) — use it for citation and editorial review
<h> homograph number in the key line; <hom> in the body is its printed counterpart
{#…#} Sanskrit text in SLP1 — a strict one-character-per-phoneme ASCII encoding; rendered to Devanāgarī or IAST (table below)
Accent characters: / = udātta; in saṃhitā quotations \ and ^ mark the anudātta bar and svarita stroke of the print
{%…%} italics of the print — in PWG that is the German definition text
¦ headword/body divider — everything before it is the lemma as printed, everything after is the article
<ls> literature source; when the source repeats, <ls n="…"> carries the silently-omitted part, so ‘9,97,1’ expands back to the full reference
<e> typographic level of the headword in the 1899 MW print: 1 = main entry with Devanāgarī, 2 = subordinate roman-only entry, 3 = run-on compound; a letter suffix (1A, 2B) = continuation segment of the same entry
<ab> abbreviation (cl., P., Ā., mfn., ifc. …); <lex> carries the gender/part-of-speech label
<lang> language label — here flagging a Prākṛt form inside the Sanskrit entry
<info> machine layer — modern enrichment absent from the print: parsed gender sets, Whitney-roots and Westergaard cross-links, verb class/pada codes. Consume these before re-deriving anything
<LEND> record terminator — records are a flat streamable sequence, one headword segment each
One printed entry ≠ one record: each sense block of MW’s ‘3. heman’ is its own record; the ¦-initial body and the <e> letter suffix (1A) mark the continuation — join on k1 + adjacency to rebuild the printed paragraph
Round-trip check: the two records above reassemble into the single print paragraph, senses joined by semicolons
Where the records come from: Monier-Williams 1899, p. 1304 — the ‘3. heman’ entry of Exhibit 3 as printed. Scan: Cologne Sanskrit-Lexicon.

The Cologne record model

All ~40 dictionaries of the Cologne Digital Sanskrit Dictionaries (CDSL) share this markup: a flat sequence of <L>…<LEND> records, headword keys in SLP1, print-faithful body markup, and a growing machine layer. The same tag inventory you see here reads PWG (German, 1855–75) and MW (English, 1899) alike — which is what makes cross-dictionary tooling possible.
