Main headword: Devanāgarī + bold transliteration — the print’s two-script convention; homograph number between them

Etymological judgment stated openly — ‘of doubtful derivation’; MW flags what it cannot derive
&c. after citations = attested widely from that point on; MW compresses where PWG enumerates
Botanical identification in Linnaean binomials — plant names are identified, not just translated (Mesua Roxburghii)
Compound stub: hema ‘in comp. for 3. heman’ opens the compound run — homograph number carried along
Compounds run on inside one paragraph: the dash replaces the first member (— kakṣa = hema-kakṣa) — hundreds of compounds per column this way
g˚ = ‘golden’: even the recurring gloss word is abbreviated to its initial inside the compound run
mf(ā)n. = adjective of three genders, feminine stem in -ā — the parenthesis gives the feminine formation
Roots as headwords: three homograph roots gup — same lemma family as PWG’s treatment, one column to compare
cl. 4. P. = present class 4, parasmaipada — Whitney-style verb classification up front

Prākṛt evidence cited inside a Sanskrit entry — Middle-Indic forms in italics with their source
Principal parts listed with grammarians’ references: perf. jugopa, fut. gopsyati, aor. agaupsīt — each form documented from Pāṇini or the literature
Derived stems get their own sense blocks: the desiderative jugupsate ‘to shun, detest’ — semantically emancipated from ‘guard’
Literal meaning in quotation marks before the lexicalised use — ifc. (‘at the end of a compound’)

How to use this page

The column re-typesets entries from the digital MW text (Cologne, csl-orig v02) following the 1899 print conventions; each label explains one element. Monier-Williams (1899) is the standard English-language Sanskrit dictionary: two-script headwords, etymological grouping, compressed citations, and very long compound runs. The lemma family (heman, gup) is the same as on the PWG specimen page — compare how the two traditions treat identical material. MW’s own transliteration (kaksha, ṛi) is modernised to IAST here; the facsimile shows the original.
