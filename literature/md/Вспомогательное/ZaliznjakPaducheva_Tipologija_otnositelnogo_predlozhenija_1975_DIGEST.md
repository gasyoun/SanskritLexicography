## What the article argues

Building directly on Émile Benveniste's 1957–58 "La phrase relative, problème de syntaxe
générale" (which showed that relative-clause markers and the markers used to build noun
phrases inside simple sentences are, in many languages, essentially the same devices), the
authors set out to construct a cross-linguistic **inventory of the syntactic functions**
that a relativizing element can bear, and of the "bundles of functions" (пучки функций)
different languages fuse into a single marker. Their method: since one and the same
conjunctive word typically performs several functions at once (linking clauses AND standing
in as a clause constituent), languages differ not just in which functions they have but in
*which functions get bundled together morphologically* — polysemy vs. homonymy of the
relativizer being a live synchronic question the article flags but sets aside for typology's
sake.

The article carefully delimits its object (relative clauses introduced by a союзное слово,
i.e. a word that is simultaneously a connective and a clause member, as opposed to plain
conjunctions or clauses referring back to a whole preceding sentence), then works through
boundary cases from Arabic, Old Russian, and elsewhere where the sentential/coordinate vs.
subordinate-clause distinction is itself unclear, arguing these marginal cases must still be
folded into the typology rather than excluded by fiat.

The core typological payoff is a three-way historical/synchronic classification of relative
pronoun systems by which more basic (non-relative) pronoun category the relativizer is
etymologically/functionally tied to:

- **Archaic type** — the subordinating element is historically an interrogative/indefinite
  pronoun (reconstructed base schema: "A man did wrong. *That one* must be punished," with
  the demonstrative doing double duty as the neutral anaphor); Indo-Iranian *yas* (Sanskrit)
  and Greek *hós* are cited as members with an unclear ultimate origin.
- **Semitic type** — the subordinator has an originally demonstrative value (Arabic
  *'alladhī*, historically built from two demonstrative elements), with personal pronouns
  doing the neutral anaphoric work.
- **Classical type** — a mixed/later category (Latin, Russian, German *der*), in several
  cases demonstrably arising by historical transformation of the archaic type.

A closing section (echoing Benveniste) argues the relative clause is syntactically
equivalent to an adjective, and surveys — with a worked Sanskrit example — how languages
vary in whether the attributive or the predicative "quality-word" construction is the
grammatically more complex one: Old Indic *Nalo balī* is identical in form for both "Nala is
mighty" and "mighty Nala," whereas Russian *bel* (predicate) vs. *bely-j* (attribute) shows
the attributive form as morphologically more complex — and the article traces exactly this
complex-attributive pattern back to a fossilized relative-clause-with-nominal-predicate
origin, using Old Persian *kāra hyā hamiçiya* ("army which [is] rebellious") and Old Indic
*Somam bhuvanasya yas patiḥ* ("Soma, of-the-world who lord" = "Soma, lord of the world") as
the historical source construction for the Balto-Slavic long-form adjective.

## Why this article, for this project

The paper is a foundational text of the Moscow school's formal syntactic typology and is
used here on two fronts: (1) as the **Russian theoretical-tradition framework for
relative-clause typology and cross-linguistic syntax** that SaLex's own research papers draw
on when situating Sanskrit *yas/ya-* relative constructions typologically (the article
itself repeatedly uses Sanskrit *yas* and worked Sanskrit examples as primary typological
data points, not just illustration); and (2) by RuTrans (the RussianTranslation subproject,
PWG→Russian dictionary translation work) as a methodological reference for how a rigorous
Russian-language syntactic-typology description handles cross-linguistic functional
bundling — directly relevant to translating PWG's German grammatical terminology and
syntactic glosses into precise Russian equivalents.
