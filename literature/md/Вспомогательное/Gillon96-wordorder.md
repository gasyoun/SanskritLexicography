INTRODUCTION
Since the beginning of the formal study of language, the ﬂexibility in word order
exhibited by languages alleged to have free word order has been a persistent
source of puzzlement, both to theorists and to observers. One such language is
Classical Sanskrit.
However, word order in Classical Sanskrit prose is not free.
That is to
say, it is not the case that any pair of words in a Sanskrit expression can be
transposed without disturbing its meaning. This is patent in the case of Sanskrit
compounds: “r¯aja-purus.a” (“king’s man”) does not mean the same as “purus.a-
r¯aja” (“man’s king”). Indeed, only in very special circumstances can two words
in a Sanskrit compound be permuted without disturbance of meaning.
The contrast in the pertinence of linear order between Sanskrit compounds,
on the one hand, and inﬂected elements making up a sentence, on the other,
is implicitly recognized in P¯an.ini’s As.t.¯adhy¯ay¯ı and explictly remarked upon by
Pata˜njali in his Mah¯abh¯as.ya. I shall abide by this distinction in what follows,
assuming phrasal and lexical syntax to be independent of one another.1
While it is true that in a Sanskrit sentence many pairs of words can be
transposed without altering its sense; many cannot. This is especially evident
in the case where one of the candidates for transposition is a particle such as

2
PHRASE MARKERS AND LINEAR ORDER
The ﬁrst person to address the problem of word order in Classical Sanskrit
within a post-structuralist linguistic framework, was J. F. Staal. Staal (1967)
thought that the degree of freedom there is in the linear order of words in
a Classical Sanskrit sentence can be characterized, not by the application of
transformations to an underlying phrase marker, ordered both hierarchically
and linearly, but by the use of phrase markers into which a suitable degree of
underspeciﬁcation of linear order is introduced, thereby obviating any appeal to
transformations. Staal conceived of these phrase markers on an analogy with
Calder Mobiles: the idea being that the sisters under each node can be freely
transposed, provided that such transposition induces no tangling. Staal called
phrase markers which tolerated this degree of underspeciﬁcation of linear order
among the sisters of a constituent “wild trees” (Staal 1967 p. 15). What Staal
did not do, and what no one else, to my knowledge, has done, is to give a
precise deﬁnition of this kind of phrase marker and to explore the empirical
consequences of analyzing Sanskrit sentences in terms of them. Some time ago,
I undertook to do just this; and it is the results of this undertaking and its
implications, both for the study of Classical Sanskrit syntax in particular, and
for syntactic theory in general, which I present here.
Staal’s basic idea is a very simple one: namely, word order is free among the
sisters of one and the same constituent. In other words, he thinks that word
order is free up to preserving constituency. (See Staal 1967 pp. 36, 64.) Thus,
for a simple Classical Sanskrit sentence such as,

Unfortunately, Staal’s idea received little notice from linguists and none
from Sanskritists. The only sustained criticism of the use of wild trees within
linguistic theory came from Emmon Bach (1975). The form of his argument is
to consider several ways of formally realizing wild trees and to show that each
such realization either leads to undue complexity or does not obviate the need
for transformations.
Clearly, the former alternative is an undesirable result;
but the latter is an undesirable result, only if the transformations required
both are independently motivated and can characterize whatever Staal’s wild
trees can characterize. But what I propose below does retain transformations.3

However, the facts characterized by transformations on the one hand and wild
trees on the other are virtually discrete: transformations are used to characterize
discontinuity within a constituent while wild trees are used to characterize the
free linear ordering of sisters within a continuous constituent. Each mechanism
has a simple formulation.
In what follows, I shall formalize Staal’s notion of a wild tree in a way
that eschews the complexities accruing to Bach’s various formalizations. Then,
I shall then show how, suitably supplemented, wild trees help us obtain an
important generalization about word order in Sanskrit, which generalization
falls to independently motivated, cross-linguistically attested transformational
treatment. I shall conclude with a discussion of how the treatment of Classical
Sanskrit is susceptible of generalization which I believe can prove useful to the
treatment of word order, not only in other languages alleged to have free word
order, but also in languages known not to.
3
FORMALIZING WILD TREES
To formalize Staal’s notion of wild trees, one must begin by making a few min-
imal, and I hope uncontroversial, assumptions about the syntax of Classical
Sanskrit. The ﬁrst is that Classical Sanskrit has the four basic lexical categories
of adjectives (A), nouns (N), prepositions (P), and verbs (V). (See Gillon and
Dash 1986.) The second is that each of these lexical categories has as its sole
phrasal projection its phrasal category. The third is that a phrase comprises
its head and any number of phrasal complements or clausal complements. The
fourth is that a clause (S)4 comprises an optional (subject) noun phrase (NP),
an optional auxiliary, and a verb phrase (VP). And the ﬁfth is that neither
parentheticals, which include vocatives and interjections, nor conjunctions par-
ticipate in the syntactic structures just outlined, though their placement in a
sentence does depend on them. These ﬁve assumptions can be encapsulated in
the following order-free categorial rules.

THE DATA
Recall that Staal’s hope was that wild trees would obviate the need for transfor-
mations to account for word order. This meant, in eﬀect, that each unambiguous
Classical Sanskrit prose sentence would be assigned only one order-free phrase
marker which conforms to the order-free categorial rules in (7). The implication
of this is that all constituents in Sanskrit are continuous: that is to say, roughly,
that words which are thought to be construed together are found together. In-
versely, a constituent is discontinuous when the least sequence of words (based
on their actual linear order) encompassing all words thought to be construed
together encompass words not thought to be part of the constituent. In the ﬁrst
example below, the constituents are thought to be continuous. In particular,
“keeps his bicycle in a garage” is continuous. In the second example, the very
same constituent is rendered discontinuous by the intervening sequence “the
eccentric man”.
(9.1) The eccentric man keeps his bicycle in a garage.
(9.2) His bicycle the eccentric man keeps in a garage.
Discontinuities such as this, as is well known, provided much of the motivation
for transformational grammar.
Now the sentences in (6) exhibit precisely the discontinuity exhibited in
(9.2): the constituent “govindam apa´syat” is rendered discontinuous by the
intervention between “govindam” and “apa´syat” of “r¯amah.”. As Staal realized,
sentences such as those in (6) are not uncommon in Classical Sanskrit and
constitute prima facie counter-examples to his hypothesis. The question arises:

is there a pattern to these discontinuities? The answer is that there appears to
be a well-deﬁned pattern, remarkable for the fact that it is so-well attested in a
variety of the world’s languages.
The evidence comprises a corpus of approximately one thousand Classical
Sanskrit prose sentences, half of which are taken from a single text 5 and half
of which are selected at random from what are regarded as the best examples
of Classical Sanskrit literature. 6
Of the thousand sentences examined, one hundred and sixty or so exhibit
some form of discontinuity, sixty of which are found in the single text and
the remaining two-thirds in the random sample.
The preponderance of the
sentences exhibiting discontinuity do so with respect to only one constituent in a
clause. In virtually every case, a non-head sister of the putatively discontinuous
constituent occurs in a clause peripheral position while the head and its other
sisters, if they exist, occur in a position which satisﬁes the assumptions made
above. That is to say, if the non-head sister in a clause terminal position were
eliminated from the sentence, no constituent would be discontinuous. Let such
a non-head sister be designated a dislocated constituent. For the most part,
the dislocated element occurs at the periphery of the clause of its sister. If it is
dislocated to the right periphery of its clause, it occurs as the last element in the
clause; and if it is dislocated to the left side of its clause, then if the dislocated
constituent is not itself the ﬁrst element in the clause, it is typically preceded
only by an interjection, vocative, conjunction, an adverbial-like constituent with
sentential scope, another dislocated element7, or some combination thereof.

5.1
MOVEMENT WITHIN PRINCIPAL CLAUSES
5.1.1
Extraposition from Subject NP
Extraposition from subject NP is familiar to those who study English. As is well-
known, extraposition in English is always to the right periphery of the clause
containing the subject NP. Moreover, not just any phrase within a subject NP
may extrapose: no element which precedes the head noun of the subject noun
phrase may extrapose; and only some of the phrases or clauses which succeed
the noun may.8
In Classical Sanskrit, none of these idiosyncracies seems to obtain. To begin
with, recall that there is no consitutent intermediate between a lexical category
and its projection. Evidence for intermediate constituency in English accrues
to several distributional regularities: ﬁrst, “one” anaphora; second, ordering;
third, extraposition itself; and fourth, co-ordination. To begin with, Classical
Sanskrit lacks any pronominal counterpart to the anaphoric “one” of English.
In addition, the evidence, and the basic assumption being explored here on
the basis of it, is that linear ordering is free with respect to the immediate
constituents of a phrase. Moreover, the very diﬀerence in extraposability among
the complements of English subject noun phrases, which is adduced as evidence
to support intermediate projections, appears to be lacking in Classical Sanskrit.
Finally, the co-ordination cases in the data appear to be silent on the matter.
As we shall see now, extraposition from subject NP in Sanskrit is far less
constrained: any modifying phrase or clause, sister to the head, may be extra-
posed but none of their properly contained constituents may be. The elements
extraposed from a noun phrase below include a noun phrase, an adjective phrase
comprising just a simple adjective, which corresponds to a determiner in En-
glish, and a participial clause. As far as can be ascertained, extraposition is not
sensitive to the choice of verb.

6.2
EXTRAPOSITION WITHIN ABSOLUTIVE CLAUSES
An absolutive, or absolute clause (AC), is distinguished by several characteris-
tics: ﬁrst, it has a subject; second, it has a verb in participial form; and third,
the subject with which the verb agrees in case, number, and gender, is in the
seventh (locative) case, or more rarely, in the sixth (genitive) case. The ex-
ceptions are a small number of idiomatic usages in which there are no overt
subjects (e.g., “tath¯a anus.t.hite”: “-it⟩being thus done”) and those instances
where a copula in the appropriate form can be supplied (e.g., “asmin ´sus.ke
-sati⟩”: “while it -is⟩dry”).11 (Speijer 1886 §365 - §372.)
Moreover, an absolute clause is a subordinate clause, in the sense that it
cannot stand on its own, but rather it must be connected to another subordi-
nating clause. The subordinating clause either is immediately adjacent to, or
completely surrounds, it. More precisely, in a clause which has a subject noun
phrase and whose subject noun phrase precedes its verb phrase, the gerundial
clause is usually found either preceding the subject noun phrase or between the
subject noun phrase and verb phrase. 12
There are other signs of the subordination of absolute clauses to their adja-
cent or surrounding clause. First, the time of the event expressed by absolute
clause’s verb is deﬁned only in relation to the time of the event expressed by the
verb of the surrounding or adjacent clause. This temporal relation is modulated
through the aspectual dimension of participial suﬃx of the absolute clause’s
verb. Second, the subject of an absolute clause cannot refer to the same thing
as the subject or object of the verb in the surrounding or adjacent clause.
Now, an absolute clause may be subordinated to any kind of clause, not only
to an independent clause,

CONCLUSION
As pointed out at the beginning of the paper, prose sentences of Classical San-
skrit are subject to constraints on linear order. This is well-known in the case
of compound formation and particle placement. It is also indisputable in the
case of the linear ordering of words from distinct clauses: words from distinct

namely, extraposition from subject position, extraposition from verb comple-
ment positions, and verb complement topicalization – all of which appear to be
clause-bounded.
It might be thought that these constraints – the free ordering of sisters and
the possible forms of discontinuity – render order within a single clause free.
But this is, in fact, not so, for otherwise the nine sentences treated in section 7

suﬃcient brevity, any linear order of its words is possible; but once its subject
