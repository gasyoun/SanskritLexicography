## What the paper argues

The fuller, English-language, peer-reviewed treatment of Russian correlative constructions
(*"whoever comes first, that one gets the best seats"*-type sentences), substantially
extending Mitrenina's own earlier 2008 Dialogue-conference squib (`55.md`, digested
separately as `55_Mitrenina_2008_DIGEST.md`). Motivation: Russian is unusually rich in
correlatives but had been excluded from Bhatt's (2003) cross-linguistic correlative-typology
list, and existing formal accounts (Bhatt's low-generation-then-movement analysis of Hindi;
Lipták's 2005 external-adjunction analysis of Hungarian) each fail to fully predict Russian
behavior.

**Core content, section by section:**
- **§2** gives a near-complete inventory of Russian XPrel–XPana correlative pronoun pairs
  (*kto/tot, čto/to, kakoj/takoj, čej/togo, gde/tam, kuda/tuda, dokuda/dotuda, kogda/togda,
  kak/tak, skol'ko/stol'ko, naskol'ko/nastol'ko, čem/tem* (comparative), *kakov/takov*),
  each illustrated, plus a note that the archaic pronoun *kotoryj* formed correlatives before
  the mid-18th century.
- **§3** applies four standard headed-relative-vs-correlative diagnostics to Russian
  (head-position flexibility, obligatory demonstrative, multi-head constructions,
  stacking). The key novel finding: **Russian allows stacking of correlative clauses**
  (attested in corpus examples), which the cross-linguistic literature (Bhatt, Lipták) had
  treated as restricted to headed relatives and impossible for correlatives in every
  language studied so far.
- **§4–5** map out which of the six logically possible Sub/Dem/Main clause orderings are
  grammatical (four are, with some construction-specific exceptions for *kakoj*- and
  comparative correlatives), and give an original **coordination** argument: Dem+Sub forms
  a constituent (can be coordinated as a unit, fronted or in-situ) while Sub+Dem cannot —
  including a detailed footnote (crediting an anonymous reviewer) on comparative-correlative
  coordination requiring a "secondary" demonstrative *tak*.
- **§6–7** revisit and extend the reconstruction and island-effect data first reported in
  the 2008 Dialogue paper (cited twice in-text as "(Mitrenina 2008)"): reconstruction facts
  are shown to be ambiguous/inconclusive for Russian, but a Complex-NP-island effect is
  robust across informant judgments.
- **§8** proposes the paper's main theoretical contribution: an **Information-Structure
  account** built on Slioussar's (2007) *relational* (accessibility/salience) IS model
  rather than categorical given/new or topic/focus notions, deriving the Sub-Dem-Main
  order (and the impossibility/markedness of other orders) from independently-motivated
  IS-driven movement, with the sentence-final emphatic particle *i* marking the most
  salient (narrow-focus) constituent.
- **§9** concludes the movement-vs.-base-generation question remains genuinely
  underdetermined for Russian by current binding-theory data, but that the IS-based account
  is compatible with either analysis and explains the attested word-order facts either way.

## Relationship to the companion Dialogue-2008 paper (`55.md`)

Not a duplicate — see the reciprocal digest (`55_Mitrenina_2008_DIGEST.md`) for the full
comparison. In short: this FDSL paper is the definitive, expanded English publication that
explicitly builds on the shorter 2008 Russian conference paper's reconstruction and
island-effect data, adding an entire pronoun-paradigm inventory, the stacking and
coordination findings, and the Slioussar-based IS formalization that the 2008 paper only
sketched informally.

Per the repo's literature index: **Russian formal syntax (FDSL conference)** and **Russian

(`RussianTranslation` subproject) as the primary citable generative-syntax source when
Russian correlative/relative-clause constructions, information-structure-driven word order,
or Slavic-linguistics terminology need grounding for translation or grammatical-annotation
