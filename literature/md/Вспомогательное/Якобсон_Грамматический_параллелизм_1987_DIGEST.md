## What the article argues

Jakobson surveys the cross-cultural scholarship on **grammatical parallelism** — the
poetic technique (opening with Gerard Manley Hopkins's 1865 dictum that "the artificial
part of poetry... reduces itself to the principle of parallelism") whereby successive
lines repeat or systematically vary phonological, morphological, syntactic, and lexical
categories, forcing the categories placed in metrically equivalent positions to be read as
semantically or grammatically equivalent. He traces the comparative tradition from Robert
Lowth's 1778 identification of biblical Hebrew *parallelismus membrorum* (synonymous,
antithetic, synthetic parallels) through Chinese parallel-prose scholarship (Davis,
Hightower's six-type taxonomy, Boodberg's "stereoscopic" reading), to the Uralic-Altaic
oral epic tradition — Steinitz's landmark "grammar of parallelism" for Karelian-Finnish
runic songs, Austerlitz on Ostyak/Vogul metrics, and parallel Turkic/Mongolic epic
material — explicitly noting (citing Gonda's *Stylistic Repetition in the Veda*, 1959) that
a similarly symmetrical, repetition-based style is abundantly attested in the **Vedas**,
though he cautions this Vedic tendency should not simply be equated with the canonical
sequential-parallelism patterns he is comparing.

Jakobson then argues that **Russian folk poetry (byliny and lyric/ritual songs) is the only
living Indo-European oral tradition** that still uses grammatical parallelism as its primary
verse-linking device, and devotes the bulk of the article to an exhaustive line-by-line
linguistic analysis of one 18th-century song from the Kirsha Danilov collection ("Ой, горе,
горе — горевáньице"), demonstrating multiple simultaneous layers of parallelism: hemistich
(line-internal) parallelism, couplet-level synonymic and antithetic parallelism (with a
careful distinction between "contradictory" and "contrary" oppositions), phonological
figures (paronomasia, etymological figures/paregmenon), morphological polyptoton, syntactic
chiasmus and anadiplosis (Russian "styk"), and a large-scale compositional symmetry between
the song's two stanzas (an infinitive-dominated, personification-building first half vs. a
finite-verb, fully personified second half). He compares this structural apparatus to a
biblical hexastich (Song of Songs 4:8) and explicitly imports the Sanskrit poetic-theory
term **dīpaka** ("condensation of expression," from Bharata's *Nāṭyaśāstra*) as the
technical name for the ellipsis pattern he finds in both the Hebrew and Russian material,
alongside citing Gonda's account of verb-ellipsis in Vedic parallel constructions. The
article closes by defending parallelism against the recurring claim that it is a mark of
"primitive," repetition-poor verbal art (invoking Herder's retort to that charge), and by
distinguishes structural/grammatical parallelism from the merely "psychological" or
content-based parallelism proposed by Veselovsky, arguing the latter's own best examples
are better explained grammatically.

## Why this article, for this project

Jakobson's theory of grammatical parallelism — and specifically his cross-linguistic
comparative method for showing how phonological, morphological, and syntactic equivalence
classes structure verse — is used here for **poetic-structure analysis of Sanskrit verse**:
the article's own comparanda already include Vedic repetition/symmetry (via Gonda) and a
borrowed Sanskrit poetic-theory term (Bharata's *dīpaka*), making it a directly applicable
methodological bridge rather than a purely Slavic-folklore case study. It supplies the
close-reading toolkit (couplet-level synonymy/antithesis, hemistich-internal parallelism,
anadiplosis, compositional symmetry across larger verse units) this project applies when
analyzing formulaic and parallelistic structure in Sanskrit verse (Vedic and epic) material.
