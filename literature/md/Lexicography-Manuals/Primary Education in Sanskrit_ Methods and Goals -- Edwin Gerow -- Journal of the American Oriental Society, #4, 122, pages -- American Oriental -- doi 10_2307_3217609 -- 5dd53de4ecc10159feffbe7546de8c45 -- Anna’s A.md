A TRUISM INDOLOGISTS OFTEN RESORT TO is that the 
culture of traditional India is in many respects situated 
vis-a-vis our own as its opposite pole. On the one hand, 
we find a system of seemingly fixed castes that deter- 
mines one's social persona as a fact of nature; on the 
other, a notion that the individual by right is autonomous 
and delegates his authority to the greater society by com- 
pact. On the one hand, a behavioral ideal that privileges 
the absence of self in all its forms and abolishes its sig- 
nificance in an infinitude of rebirths; on the other, a view 
that "self-interest" is the motor of every success and that 
this life is the sole theater of enjoyments. On the one 
hand, an anhistorical civilization that is preoccupied with 
transcendental, and definitely non-dateable, matters; on 
the other, a record-keeping culture that has made "infor- 
mation" the be-all and end-all of existence. The list can 
be quite long. Traditional Indian culture, aspects of which 
survive even today, is still largely inherited from one's 
parents and may be said to be, in that sense, "innate," 
"essential." It is very unmodern, as no one needs to be re- 
minded, in these days of "finding yourself" and shopping 
for "culture" like you shop for a better Aromatherapy or 
domestic "conjoint." Now, in my view-unlike 
much

diagnostic of the whole, something akin to a litmus test: 
education-the education of the young. Another truism is 
that every culture replicates itself in its system of edu- 
cation-already a sad enough commentary on our own.

At each of the three lower levels of instruction in the 
Mysore curriculum is enshrined a distinctive methodol- 
ogy of instruction, which has come to be associated with 
a subject matter pertinent to it. In rigorous sequence, the 
methods build upon one another, not only in achieving 
mastery of content, but in cultivating various prelimi- 
nary skills and attitudes, both intellectual and ethical, of 
importance to the ultimate product: the pandita. To a 
large but diminishing extent, these methods appear to be 
retained intact from pre-college modes of instruction, 
and constitute one of the most viable links with the "tra- 
dition" we have left. The task I have set myself today is 
to describe for you, not so much those methods, which 
in their general shape are well enough known, as their 
coherence in a system. 
(1) Elementary (Prathama, or "first") level. The key- 
word here is "memorization." The eight-year-old begin- 
ner faces a curriculum which poses as its first and unique 
problem a language that is alien to him. He neither knows 
what he is to learn, nor has he the means to learn it. Two 
approaches alone to this Platonic problem are possible 
(apart from recollection, at least): either the student's na- 
tive language is utilized in order to rationalize the target 
language, or reliance is had on mechanical and repetitive 
drills intended to inculcate an ability, before it is put to 
any use at all. It is this latter approach, akin perhaps to 
anamnesis, that so distinguishes the Sanskrit methodol- 
ogy of elementary learning.l1 The Sanskritic emphasis on 
memory and recall differs from Plato's, however, in one 
essential aspect-it presumes a child who has yet to 
reach the age of discretion, and therefore it has elimi- 
nated nearly every appeal to understanding in the mem- 
orizational process-it is the sole method employed at 
this early stage of study. 
Since the mastery of Sanskrit alone authorizes entry 
into advanced levels of traditional study, it is hardly sur- 
prising that Sanskrit alone constitutes the sole concern of 
the elementary curriculum-and it is taught in a way as 
to anchor it firmly and to get the job done as quickly as 
possible. Anyone who has taught Sanskrit through West- 
ern methodologies will recognize how unsuccessful they 
are-how much effort goes into how little result-but of 
course other variables are at play here, as well. The tra- 
ditional student proceeds with remarkable rapidity and 
with solid acquisitions. Thus it will be seen that much 
contemporary criticism of the methods of Sanskrit study 
is in fact misplaced: the method works remarkably well, 
given its goals. Criticism, if any, should be leveled at 
the goals-perhaps the exclusivity of concern with San- 
skrit-but that is another matter, and really comes down 
to the culturally myopic question of whether we really 
need pundits at all. 
Rote memorization is exercised at the Prathama level 
in three contexts fundamental to language study, and 
which bear directly on the skills that will be inculcated 
(with more insight) at the next level: lexicon, metrics, 
and grammar. These three contexts exhaust the areas 
where automatic selection may be thought to operate in 
this or any language system; memorization discovers, 
then reinforces that automaticity. Dictionary learning is 
considered basic to everything else and, indeed, consti- 
tutes the core of the "literary" curriculum. Instruction 
takes the form of memorizing lists of near synonyms, 
each word serving to anchor its neighbors in recall. Mem- 
orization of verse-accompanied only by an occasional 
gloss in the student's native language-cultivates both 
pronunciation and respect for the phrasal integrity of the 
language. Finally, grammatical memorization takes the 
form of pattern drills (quite like those that were once 
standard for Latin students) that inculcate respect for the 
limitations placed on syntax in any utterance. Let us take 
up each, briefly, in sequence.

(A) Lexicon. The dictionary most commonly memo- 
rized (in Mysore and elsewhere) is that of Amara, very 
likely the oldest of the comparable Sanskrit lexica,12 and 
the model for those that follow. The author is variously 
dated from the fourth to the sixth centuries and is often 
said to be a Buddhist, but, if so, it is certainly surprising 
that he devotes the quasi-totality of the svargavarga 
("section on heaven," 1.11-142) to Hindu divinities! It 
seems a reasonable assumption that the work was com- 
posed for purposes very like those to which it is put now- 
adays, which permits us to presume at least a millennium 
and a half of amarapatha. The term "dictionary" is, of 
course, misleading here; for no definitions are provided 
and the arrangement resembles that of a thesaurus. The 
entire work (in the NSP edition) consists of 2,989 half 
verses of sixteen syllables each, including numerous in- 
terpolated verses. Circa 13,000 terms are referenced.'3 
Amara's dictionary is itself composed in metrical 
form, an evident aid to memorization-the ubiquitous 
sloka, whose monotonous eight-syllable cadence per-

Let us now consider the classroom methods used to 
master Amara. The principle involved, abhyasa, "repe- 
tition," is a technique clearly borrowed from Vedic study. 
Also derivative of Vedic study is the division of the text 
into blocks, which are learnt seriatim. Circa twenty verses 
(forty half-slokas) are committed to memory during each 
fortnight (paksa, by which divisions of the lunar month 
the rhythm of study in the pathasala is still regulated), 
following exactly the NSP text of Amara. Only the sec- 
tion on plants (vanausadhi) is not learnt in order, but de- 
ferred until the fourth year. At this rate, approximately 
four years are required for learning the entire text (as-

The net effect of these various drills is to create the 
basis for a wide reading vocabulary by evoking words in 
automatic association with quasi-synonyms, and to em- 
phasize the notion that the text is little but the sequence 
(of words, verses, etc.), the integrity of which is a func- 
tion of continuous repetition--abhyasa. Learning San- 
skrit in this way is thus more akin to learning a musical 
score than it is to learning a language. To a pundit, our 
method of emphasizing always "meaning" seems a bit 
like beginning your musical study with a Mozart sonata. 
If we presume that the study of Amara (or something 
similar) has always been an aspect of the elementary 
curriculum, and that the student's Veda was in all likeli- 
hood being memorized simultaneously, we see how the 
congruence of methods may aid in the memorization of 
both "texts"-and (evidently) how Vedic study might 
itself serve as a model for study in general. 
In choosing the dictionary as the prime example of 
"rote memorization," we can however exaggerate the 
actual intractability of the dictionary's content. As noted 
above, a good many of the words being memorized are 
already known to the student in their vernacular dress- 
especially to a Cannarese speaker, whose language has 
borrowed so extensively from Paninian Sanskrit. The 
panca of paicasara would likely be familiar already 
through pancatantra, pancayat, etc. Furthermore, the 
semantic categories which organize the dictionary often 
"explain" by the simplest principles of association those 
words not easily recognized. It is true that no word is an- 
alyzed grammatically-that is the office of the next stage 
of study-but its reference is by these tokens often far 
from unclear. In this respect, the study of Amara (rather 
than a voyage into the absurd, as it might seem to us) 
constitutes for the traditional student his first excursion 
into meaning-especially in relation to his prior Vedic 
study. That language is not only largely unintelligible as 
such (sathhitapatha) but has been made even more so by 
the purposive deformations of the text designed to an- 
chor it well (kramapatha, etc.). 
(B) Grammar. It is premature, of course, to speak of 
what goes on here as "grammar"-anymore than the pre- 
vious matter dealt, sensu stricto, with "dictionary." Yet, 
"isolation of speech elements" (vyakarana), conditioned 
within the limits of abhyasa, as a method, does indeed 
characterize what is here effected-though, again, analy- 
sis is as such absent. We, of course, would consider what 
goes on here as simply "drilling": the drills are of two 
sorts-one emphasizing inflection, the other syntax. But 
the most ingenious aspect of the Indian method is its 
avoidance of parsing as the ground of either drill, even 
that of syntax, as we will see. The grammata of speech, 
as it were, are identified and taught by rote, before they 
are themselves subjected to analysis-before they are 
provided with a "grammar."

nacy, fatal to action. And, as noted above, the grammar- 
ians attribute the same architectonic quality to the verbal 
predicate, which thus serves to define and to provoke the 
complex akanksa which is the single sentence. 
The Sanskrit term visesana 'qualification' is used in the 
general sense of any word element subordinate to an- 
other, by whatever mode of subordination: that quite 
abstract relationship is termed visesanavisesyabhava. In 
the general theory of syntax espoused by the Vaiyakara- 
nas, all elements of the sentence are considered subordi- 
nate to the verbal predicate, typically a finite verb. These 
relations are expressed via the karakas, or ideal cases, 
prathama, etc., which the "real" case endings of the 
language instantiate. Reordering the sentence will then 
specify not only the hierarchy of subordination but the 
closeness of relationship to the verb as foundation. The 
basic frame of each ideal sentence is the same: subject 
or "topic" (farthest from the verb, normally expressing 
agency24), oblique cases (usually in order: locative, in- 
strumental, ablative, dative), the direct object ("most 
closely related" of the cases, as determining one of the 
fundamental categories of the verb, transitive or intransi- 
tive), then the verb (as predicate, expressing an action25). 
Each component is capable of being further qualified- 
by adjectives "agreeing" with a head noun or by a noun 
in the genitive (typically, the "case" of nominal subordi- 
nation, and hence not a "case" at all), or by an indeclin- 
able (avyaya) or adverbial element, modifying the verbal 
idea immediately. The padanvaya serves as a basis for 
this more refined analysis, placing the visesana, qualifi- 
cation, before the visesya, item qualified, an order of pre- 
cedence that is also reflected, as can be seen, in the ideal 
sentence as such. Students at this level are thus able to 
"build" a sentence out of its component parts according to

The above "derivation" is, it must be emphasized, just 
an outline of immediately relevant steps. Other, even 
more general, rules, such as those defining affixes, and cer- 
tain interpretational rules, which serve as traffic control, 
have been ignored, even though they have occasion. The 
example is sufficient, however, to establish just how dif- 
ferent the method of prakriya is from that of paradigm 
memorization. It makes obvious the sense in which no 
rule may be invoked by him who does not already know 
all the rules-and why their memorization is a precondi- 
tion of panditya. Thus, the student who becomes more 
adept with each "derivation" progressively finds himself 
in command of the entire corpus of rules-the entire 
grammar. And, sadly, it also makes clear why there are so 
few pundits nowadays, for I would not want it thought 
that the pitiful attempt above to derive a single form with 
the aid of text and the written word is or can be any sub- 
stitute for the genuine article. 
We are now ready to discuss (and illustrate) issues of 
greater methodological interest which confront the stu- 
dent thus practicing or enacting his grammar-issues 
that, in fact, serve as a bridge to the upper levels of gram- 
matical instruction, beyond the Sahitya level, where the 
grammar as a system of rules becomes the chief focus of 
inquiry. If the operation (prakriya) of the system could be 
completely automated, such that all correct forms, and no 
incorrect ones (and presumably, thus, all correct sentences, 
etc.) could be established entirely through the orderly ap- 
plication of rules general and specific, our discussion 
would have ended. (And, presumably, no "higher level" 
grammatical study need be undertaken.) But either be- 
cause of the inherent complexity of grammatical systems, 
or the obtuseness of Panini and his successors, that desir- 
able state of affairs has not (yet) been realized for San- 
skrit grammar. Cases abound in which the sense of the 
sutras must be qualified in order to permit their operation 
in the proper order, or where two sutras, even in terms of 
the ideal of automaticity outlined above, appear to apply 
in the wrong order, or to apply simultaneously, with no 
way of deciding their applicability. 
We introduced earlier the example of vrksebhyah 
because it also illustrates this further problem in very 
simple terms. The reader may already have noticed, as 
does every student of sutrapatha, that two sitras apply 
equally and simultaneously in the derivational condition 
*vrksa-bhyah, obtained, as will be recalled, by selecting 
the dative plural from the list of twenty-one endings.

jhaly et [P. 7.3.103], to produce the desired replacement

the stem vowel a, instead of replacing it, producing an 
apasabda, an "undesirable form," *vrksd-bhyah. The sys- 
tem, of course, cannot accept such indeterminacy. Fur- 
thermore, whichever rule is first applied, the conditions 
for the application of the other are thereby abolished 
(for both cancel the stated condition that the stem end in 
short a); and so it cannot be maintained that the order of 
application is of no importance. Realizing that both rules 
in fact apply simultaneously, in terms of the notions of 
generality and specificity mentioned earlier, the student
