analysis of “compounds” with ādi-
https://doi.org/10.1515/flih-2018-0005
Abstract: This paper provides a new description of nominal compounds featur-
ing the noun ādi- ‘beginning’ in Sanskrit. When used as second member of a
compound, ādi- is commonly translated as ‘etcetera’ and the resulting com-
pound displays a number of semantic and morphological oddities, as observed
in reference grammars. Based on a thorough corpus analysis, we show that ādi-
compounds are in fact associated with different functions, and that among other
things they can be employed for the task of on-line categorization. Specifically,
we argue that they can also encode ad hoc categories. In the second part of the
paper, we describe the diachronic process whereby ādi-compounds developed
into ad hoc category markers. In so doing, we adopt the perspective of con-
structionalization and suggest, against the traditional analysis of these forms as
compounds, that when meaning ‘etcetera’, ādi- behaves as an affixoid. In this
respect, the findings of this paper contribute to the general understanding of the
diachronic typology of ad hoc category markers.
Keywords: Sanskrit, nominal compounding, ad hoc categories, list constructions,
constructionalization

2 Theoretical background
2.1 Ad hoc categories: function and encoding
Categorization is a basic cognitive process of human beings that plays a key role
in how speakers learn, use, and shape language (cf. Taylor 1995). So far,
scholars have mostly focused their attention on standard categories, that is,
conventionally established categories that are stored in the speaker’s mind and
are referred to by dedicated linguistic tags. Moreover, since Rosch’s ground-
breaking studies on the prototype effect (e.g. Rosch 1973), much attention has
been paid to the structuring of categories and the internal relationship between
their members (see Croft and Cruse 2004: Chap. 4).
In more recent times, mostly thanks to the work of Barsalou (1983, 2010),
investigations have started on another type of categories, which can be labelled
ad hoc categories (Mauri 2014). Unlike conventional categories, these are built
on-line in discourse, their interpretation heavily depends on the context, and
they are not stored in long-term memory. From a linguistic point of view, they
are not accessed through lexical items, but require encoding through more
complex strategies.
Building on Barsalou’s work, Mauri (2014, 2017) discusses how the creation
of ad hoc categories is an on-line process that involves three elements: one or
more explicit exemplar(s), additional implicit members, and a superordinate
category, which subsumes both the explicit and the implicit members. Based
on the mention of an explicit exemplar, ad hoc categories are built through an

Semantically, based on the relationship established between the members,

(1)

All the examples in (1) instantiate ad hoc categories, but they differ in their
internal structure. The category in (1a) can be defined as a set, as the items are
associated to a conventional inventory of entities, as in the case of the ingre-
dients of a baking recipe. By contrast, the category in (1b) can be described as a
class, since its members are related through similarity and, unlike what happens
with sets, they are interchangeable, that is, they are not necessarily elicited as
co-occurring. Finally, in (1c), the category is construed as a frame, as its
members are sub-events associated with a specific script.
Languages resort to different strategies for the encoding of ad hoc cate-
gories, ranging from specific plural markers to more analytic strategies such as
the English general extender and something (Mauri 2014). For reasons of space,
we do not discuss the various strategies here, but we return on this issue in
Section 3.3, in which we attempt a classification of ādi-compounds within the
typology laid out by Mauri (2014, 2017) and Mauri and Sansò (2018).

In reference grammars of the language, which are partly based on ancient
grammatical descriptions, compounds are sorted out into different classes, based
on both formal and functional properties (cf. Wackernagel 1905: 149–307;
Whitney 1889: 480–515).
The first class is constituted by dependent determinative compounds (Skt.
tatpuruṣa-compounds), in which a dependency relation is established between
the two members (cf. Eng. schoolbus), as in e.g. rāja-putra- ‘son of a king’
(genitive). The semantic head of these compounds is the second member, that
also determines the inflectional features (e.g. gender) of the entire compound.
The second class is made up by descriptive, or appositive, determinative
compounds (Skt. karmadhāraya-compounds). In this case, the first member
predicates a property of the second one (cf. Eng. steamboat), which likewise is
the head of the compound and determines its inflectional features. Compounds
of this type include the combination of an adjective plus a noun, as in mahā-
rāja- ‘great king’, or of two nouns rāja-rṣi- ‘king-sage’, i.e. ‘a king who is a
sage’. In the third place, compounds can be of the copulative, or coordinative,
type (Skt. dvandva-compounds). In this case two or more nouns can be com-
bined in a coordinative fashion, as in e.g. dyā́vā-pr̥thivī́- ‘heaven and earth’.
The gender of copulative compounds is determined by their last member and
they display either dual or plural number (Whitney 1889: 485).
The last class of compounds discussed in descriptive grammars consists of
possessive exocentric compounds (Skt. bahuvrīhi-compounds). In contrast with
the other types of compounds, the denotatum of possessive compounds is not a
hyponym of one of their elements, but refers to an external head (cf. Eng.
redhead), which also determines the inflectional features of the compound itself.
Strictly speaking possessive compounds should not be viewed as a class on their
own, but rather as a specific syntactic configuration, i.e. dependency on an
external head, which is available to the three kinds of compounds discussed
above. For instance, the descriptive determinative compound br̥had-aśva- ‘a
great horse’, can be used attributively with reference to an external head, as
in br̥had-aśvaḥ‘he whose horses are great’.

In spite of the similarity between the two, we argue that category and list
ādi-compounds differ in several semantic features, reflecting more general
semantic distinctions observed for other list constructions (cf. Bonvino et al.
2009; Kahane and Pietrandrea 2012; Mauri 2014, 2017). Some of the differences
have already been discussed in Section 3.1, and we will recapitulate them here
for clarity’s sake.
The first difference has to do with the relationship among the members. In
list compounds, members are put together because they are associated with a
list, irrespective of any shared property among them. Conversely, in category
compounds all members are tied by some salient common property, which also
enables the inferential process leading to the on-line creation of the category
itself.
In addition, list compounds are exhaustive, i.e. they refer to a finite number
of members, which are also ordered in a fixed sequence, whereas category
compounds are non-exhaustive, i.e. speakers can infer a potential non-finite
number of further members, which are not conceived as placed in a fixed order.
Interestingly, whereas lists can be either collective or approximative, i.e. the
members are definite and are elicited as either co-occurring or as mutually
alternative, in category compounds the members are indefinite and are not
necessarily elicited as co-occurring. Consequently, whereas the denotation of
the former corresponds to the sum of the denotations of its members, in the
latter the denotation corresponds to the hypernym of the members. In other
words, list compounds display a member focus, whereas category compounds
display a category focus (cf. Ariel 2016).

3.4 Diachrony of ādi-compounds
As we have discussed in the previous sections, ādi-compounds can be synchroni-
cally assigned at least three different functions, and their morphosyntactic status is
somewhat unclear, especially when used non-attributively. In this section, we
attempt a diachronic explanation of the synchronic variation observed in the data.
The diachrony of ādi-compounds has been briefly touched upon by
Wackernagel (1905), whose remarks on the issue remain highly insightful.
According to him, ādi-compounds started out in the use that we have labelled
list compounds and only subsequently developed into category compounds. In
the remainder of this section, we elaborate on this point, and show that this
scenario proves to be essentially correct. We argue that this process can be
described as a process of grammaticalization, or better as one of ‘constructio-
nalization’ (Hüning and Booij 2014a, 2014b), and that the new functions
acquired by ādi-compounds constitute the result of the conventionalization of
contextual functions which were associated with the construction by speakers in
specific bridging contexts (Heine 2002).
The onset of the process can be tracked down to the lexical use of ādi-
compounds in attributive constructions, as shown in example (7). In this con-
struction, the compound is used attributively to modify a lexical head that
indicates some sort of process or event. Semantically, the compound profiles
the onset of the event on a temporal line, ensuing from the lexical meaning of
ādi- ‘beginning’.

which list compounds are issued out of lexical compounds. Note that at this

member. This motivates the rise of non-attributive list constructions, and the
development of the meaning ‘X and the rest’.

further that based on the lexical meaning of ādi-, one has to assume that the
collective list meaning is the first one to develop, and that the construction was
occasionally extended to approximative lists later on, as discussed for (10).

grammaticalization (cf. Hopper and Traugott 2003). Grammatical changes in word
formation patterns have been a major topic in grammaticalization studies, as nouns
involved in compounds evolving into derivational markers constitute a typical
example of semantic bleaching, decategorialization, and increased morphological
cohesion. A case in point is the well-known development whereby the Old High
German noun līh ‘body’ eventually developed into an adjectival suffix in Modern
German [viz. -lich] (Hüning and Booij 2014a: 581). In recent times, scholars have
pointed out how describing these processes as instantiating grammaticalization in
traditional terms runs into several problems, especially when it comes to the
relationship with lexicalization processes, and have therefore suggested that these
changes should be viewed as instantiating constructionalization instead (Traugott
and Trousdale 2013: 168–182; Hüning and Booij 2014a). Constructionalization, either
lexical or grammatical, can be broadly defined as a process whereby constructions,
including abstract schemes such as word-formation patterns, undergo changes in
either their formal or their semantic components, and in the pairing between the
two, affecting the productivity, the compositionality, and the schematicity of the
construction. In such a process, the context in which constructions first occur is
crucial in determining the possibility and the direction of the change, and analogical
reasoning plays a key role in the expansion of newly created constructions.

We suggest that the development of ādi-compounds can be profitably
described in terms of constructionalization. At the beginning of the process we
find the construction [[N]1+[ādi-]] ↔[SEM1 as beginning] that instantiates a
more schematic word-formation schema [[N]1+[N]2] ↔[SEM1 as SEM2]. In this
phase, the meaning of the construction is entirely compositional, as it can be
derived from the sum of the meanings of the items in isolation. In certain
bridging contexts, as discussed above, this construction is enriched with new
meanings, which are increasingly associated with the construction. These new
functions are not entirely predictable from the components of the construction,
and more specifically some of them turn out to be incompatible with the lexical
meaning of ādi- ‘beginning’, so that the construction as a whole loses semantic
compositionality. It is however too simplistic to describe this process as a
semantic bleaching undergone by ādi- in isolation, as also suggested by the
fact that ādi- could still be used freely with its lexical meaning elsewhere.
Rather, the process at play can be better described as one of constructionaliza-
tion: the entire construction in which ādi- occurs is associated to new meanings
in specific discourse contexts, and, consequently, ādi- becomes associated to the
meaning ‘etcetera’ only when occurring within this specific construction.
This semantic shift is paired with two changes in the structure of
category ādi-compounds. On the one hand, for the reasons discussed above,
non-attributive usages of category ādi-compounds increase in frequency, leading
to further entrenchment of the [[N]1+[ādi-]] ↔[SEM1 and the like] construction.
Also, the construction broadens its functional scope, as beside frames, category
ādi-compounds came to indicate classes and sets as well. On the other hand,
one observes changes in the internal structure of the construction, as evidenced
by gender assignment. As we have remarked in Section 3.4, in category ādi-
compounds the construction itself acquires a new specification, that is, that the
gender of the whole construction is co-indexed with the last noun compounded
with ādi-, rather than being co-indexed with ādi-, which would be the expected
behavior of gender selection in true compounds. This suggests that, as a con-
sequence of the loss of the semantic transparency of the construction, the
internal structure of ādi-compounds was progressively altered, with ādi- partly
losing is independent word status and acquiring the behavior of an affixoid, i.e.
a “compound constituent[s] with an affix-like behavior” (Hüning and Booij
2014b: 77). In other words, the [[N]1+[ādi-]] construction meaning ‘X etcetera’
loses its morphosyntactic status of a full-fledged compound and becomes a
derivational construction of the type [N1-ādi-].

functions are derived from lexical/grammatical ones, follows a well-known cline
of linguistic change termed subjectification by Traugott (2010). As Traugott puts

purely lexical meaning, and their components retain their referential value.

develop. In specific discourse contexts, these new functions are taken as more
salient by speakers and can be further conventionalized as part of the construc-

discourse functions, textual functions such as list ādi-compounds with anapho-

in compliance with Traugott’s (2010) subjectivity cline.
In the case of ādi-compounds, constructionalization and subjectification can
be thought of as two sides of the same diachronic process, but they have
different bearing for the diachronic typology of ad hoc category markers. On
the one hand, constructionalization, which includes decrease of transparency
and semantic compositionality as well as the structural reorganization of the
construction, is a source-oriented and construction-specific process. By this, we
mean that the outcome of the process can be explained by taking into account
the specific semantic and formal properties of the source construction in which
ādi- originally occurred. On the other hand, subjectification, conceived as the
development of speaker-oriented functions, is a target-oriented process, as it can
be explained by considering the final stage of the development of ādi-com-
pounds. In this sense, this feature of the development of ādi-compounds is
more generally shared by ad hoc category markers as a functional class (cf.
Overstreet 2014 on the grammaticalization of general extenders).
4 Conclusions
In this paper, we have described a strategy for the encoding of ad hoc categories
in Sanskrit, that is, compound nouns featuring the simplex noun ādi- ‘beginning’.
Keeping in line with the traditional description of these compounds, we have
shown how they synchronically display different functions. By grounding our
analysis on recent works on the semantics of list and category constructions, we
were able to spell out the difference between ādi-compounds referring to lists and
the ones referring to categories in more detail, showing how they display different
discourse functions, and backing up our interpretation with evidence from their
behavioral properties as gathered from the corpus. Specifically, by framing the
