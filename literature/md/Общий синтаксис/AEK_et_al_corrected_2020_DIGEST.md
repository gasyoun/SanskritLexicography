## What the book is

A 672-page multi-author Russian-language university textbook, *Введение в науку о языке*
("Introduction to the Science of Language"), written for students of the "Fundamental and
Applied Linguistics" (ФиПЛ/ТиПЛ) program at the Department of Theoretical and Applied
Linguistics, Faculty of Philology, Lomonosov Moscow State University — the oldest such
department in Russia (founded 1960/1961). The book was conceived by **Alexander Evgenevich
Kibrik** (1939–2012, department head 1992–2012) as an outgrowth of the "Введение в
специальность" ("Introduction to the Specialty") course he taught to first-year students;
he did not live to finish it. It was completed collectively by **23 authors** — department
staff and alumni — and published to mark what would have been Kibrik's 80th birthday,
under the compiling editorship of **Olga V. Fedorova** and **Sergey G. Tatevosov** (per the
editors' preface, signed "Ольга Федорова, Сергей Татевосов, февраль 2019 г."). The book
opens with Kibrik's own 1991 encyclopedia article "Язык" ("Language," originally written
for the *Лингвистический энциклопедический словарь*) as its introduction.

The editors position it explicitly as the modern, program-specific successor to two older
general linguistics textbooks still in use in Russia — Reformatsky's *Введение в
языковедение* (1947/last rev. 1967) and Maslov's *Введение в языкознание* (1975/1987) —
both of which they judge outdated for a program with this much emphasis on cognitive
science, corpus/computational methods, and discourse analysis, fields that barely existed
or did not exist in their current form when those two books were last revised.

## Structure — six parts, 32 continuously numbered chapters, plus a combined bibliography

**Введение (Introduction).** Language (А. Е. Кибрик) — the book's opening statement of what
"language" is as an object of study (sign system vs. individual ethnic languages; idiolect/
говор/dialect/language as social forms; language change and genetic relatedness, including a
mention of the Nostratic hypothesis).

**Часть I. Теория языка (Theory of Language)** — levels of linguistic structure: 1. Фонетика
и фонология (Arkhipov, Iosad, Staroverov); 2. Морфология (Tatevosov); 3. Синтаксис
(Lyutikova); 4. Дискурс (A. A. Kibrik) — sampled directly: defines discourse as the unity of
communicative process and resulting text, surveys its intellectual lineage (Boas's
ethnolinguistics, the Geneva and Prague schools, Russian Formalism), and lays out discourse
taxonomy, global/local structure, and discourse-level influence on smaller units as the
chapter's three research questions; 5. Семантика (Kobozeva, Tatevosov).

**Часть II. Язык и познание (Language and Cognition)** — seven chapters: 6. Лингвистика в
контексте когнитивных наук; 7. Когнитивная лингвистика; 8. Психолингвистика;
9. Усвоение родного языка; 10. Усвоение второго языка; 11. Нейролингвистика;
12. Невербальная коммуникация (prosody, gesture, gaze).

**Часть III. Языковое многообразие (Language Diversity)** — 13. Языки мира и языковое
разнообразие (Kibrik, Koryakov); 14. Сравнительно-историческое языкознание (Burlak) —
sampled directly: covers internal vs. external reconstruction, productivity/archaism
diagnostics, morphophonological alternations as fossilized sound change, and reconstructing
proto-culture from proto-lexicon, with a worked Sanskrit-vs-Slavic archaism contrast
(consonantism vs. vocalism) among its examples; 15. Лингвистическая типология (Belyaev) —
sampled directly: holistic 19th-century classification (isolating/agglutinating/flexional)
through Greenberg-era implicational universals to modern typological method (data sources,
sampling, semantic maps); 16. Социолингвистика (Daniel); 17. Кросс-культурная прагматика
(Bergelson).

**Часть IV. Методы (Methods)** — seven chapters on linguistic methodology: 18. Интроспекция;
19. Эксперимент; 20. Полевая лингвистика (Kibrik); 21. Корпусная лингвистика (Toldova);
22. Моделирование; 23. Транскрибирование устной речи (Podlesskaya); 24. Квантитативные
методы (Toldova).

**Часть V. Прикладная и компьютерная лингвистика (Applied and Computational Linguistics)** —
seven chapters, the section of most direct relevance to a lexicography project: 25.
Лексикография (Kobozeva) — dictionary-making as an applied discipline; 26. Документация
языков (Kibrik) — language documentation methodology; 27. Алфабетизация (Alpatov) —
orthography/script design for previously unwritten languages; 28. Транслитерация и
транскрипция (Koryakov); 29. Автоматическая обработка звучащей речи (Krivnova);
30. Автоматическая обработка текста (Toldova, Bonch-Osmolovskaya); 31. Извлечение
информации из текста (Toldova) — automatic content/information extraction.

**Часть VI. История языкознания (History of Linguistics)** — 32. История языкознания
(Alpatov) — linguistic traditions from the 1st millennium BCE to the present, closing the
book.

**Литература** — a single combined, alphabetized (Cyrillic then Latin) bibliography running
roughly 70+ printed pages, cited from every chapter; a substantial reference resource in its
own right covering discourse analysis, psycholinguistics, typology, historical linguistics,
computational linguistics, and general linguistic theory.

## Why this project uses it

Per this repo's own project index, the book's primary use here is as a **general
methodology reference — not Sanskrit-specific**: a comprehensive, current (2019) Russian
university linguistics textbook spanning phonetics through computational linguistics,
consulted by SaLex (this repo's own research papers) and by the RussianTranslation
(RuTrans) subproject when Russian-language terminology, chapter structure, or bibliographic
citations for a general-linguistics concept are needed. Its Lexicography (ch. 25),
Documentation (ch. 26), and Corpus Linguistics (ch. 21) chapters bear most directly on this
project's own dictionary-standardization work; the remaining chapters serve as background/
citation support rather than being drawn on directly.
