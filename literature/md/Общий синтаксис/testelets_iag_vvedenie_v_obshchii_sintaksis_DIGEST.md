## What the book is

A ~800-page (plus ~50pp. of literature/index/abbreviations back matter), two-part
introductory *general* syntax textbook — not Russian-specific, though most illustrative
examples are drawn from Russian, with substantial English, Georgian, Japanese, Italian,
and other cross-linguistic data throughout. Structured in two parts of roughly equal
weight: **Part 1, "Синтаксическое описание" (Syntactic description)** — a
framework-neutral, empirically driven survey of the descriptive apparatus any syntactic
theory has to account for; and **Part 2, "Синтаксические теории" (Syntactic theories)** —
a chapter-by-chapter tour of the major 20th-century syntactic frameworks. An introduction
covers the subject matter and history of syntactic research and situates the textbook's
aims (with a memorable run of five explanatory "metaphors" for what syntactic structure
is: a bunch of keys, an army, a matryoshka/turtle-pyramid, a chessboard, movement in
space).

## Part 1 — Syntactic description (Ch. I–IX)

- **Ch. I, Word and sentence: dependency structure** — dependency relations, heads and
  dependents (grammatical boundedness, linear order, phonetic fusion, endocentricity,
  morphosyntactic locus), the verb's central position, types of syntactic relations,
  projectivity, and the limits of dependency-structure representations.
- **Ch. II, Constituent structure and phrasal categories** — constituency tests
  (non-syntactic and syntactic: separability, adverb behavior, the "pied-piping effect,"
  pro-forms), obligatoriness of phrasal categories, and a comparison of dependency vs.
  constituency structures (including the determiner-phrase problem). Sampled directly:
  worked constituency trees for recursive NP embedding and multiply-nested infinitival
  complement chains (a five-fold-nested Karcevsky example).
- **Ch. III, Valency of the word** — semantic vs. syntactic valency and government
  patterns, limits on participant number, obligatory/optional/unrealizable arguments,
  actants vs. circonstants, adjuncts, embedded clauses as actants/circonstants, strong vs.
  weak nominal circonstants, secondary and spurious actants, semantic roles and role
  inventories, perspective, and a survey of verb role-types.
- **Ch. IV, Sentence and clause** — what makes a sentence a sentence; sentential paradigm
  (modality, mood, tense, predicate-agreement categories; question formation); copular and
  existential sentences; nominative and elliptical (incomplete) sentences; coordination vs.
  subordination of clauses; non-finite dependent constructions.
- **Ch. V, Syntactic "invisibles"** — the case for null elements: null subjects with
  infinitives and other non-finite Russian clauses, null copula/auxiliary forms, null
  infinitive markers in English, PRO (with control theory, floating quantifiers in
  infinitival/gerundial/nominal constructions), anaphoric null pro (sampled directly: the
  pro-drop contrast between English/German/French vs. Italian/Spanish/Russian, weather
  verbs, extraposed clausal subjects), and indefinite-personal/impersonal null subjects in
  Russian.
- **Ch. VI, The subject** — grammatical-relations priority; morphological, control
  (coordinate-reduction control, reflexive-reference control, PRO/pro control in various
  constructions, adjectival predicate agreement, plural-action verb selection), and
  transformational (genitive-of-negation, distributive po-phrase, PRO conversion,
  nominalization genitive/adjective, verb compounding) diagnostics for subjecthood in
  Russian; subjecthood in English; the universality-of-subject problem.
- **Ch. VII, Means of expressing syntactic relations** — selective features, morphological
  dependency, agreement/congruence (including non-local agreement across NP and clause
  boundaries, Keenan's agreement principle), government (Keenan's government principle,
  strong/weak government), word order and adjacency.
- **Ch. VIII, Diathesis, voice, actant derivation** — defining diathesis/voice; types of
  passive constructions and their functions; reflexive and reciprocal; marginal diatheses;
  parametric diathesis; actant derivation (valency-splitting, morphological categories
  introducing secondary actants).
- **Ch. IX, Communicative categories** — theme/rheme, thetic sentences and rheme
  expansion, given/new, contrastiveness, verification, emphasis, topic, empathy focus.

## Part 2 — Syntactic theories (Ch. X–XVI)

- **Ch. X, Explanation in linguistics, or what is grammatical theory** — goals of
  grammatical theory, the heuristic value of intuition, empirical universals, the
  explanatory base, the hypothesis↔fact cycle, formal grammars.
- **Ch. XI, Generative grammar: from rules to constraints** — competence/performance,
  the poverty-of-stimulus argument, the "standard theory" (phrase-structure rules,
  the need for transformations, the transformational component — inversion, affix-hopping,
  do-support, dative movement, extraposition, negative transformation, and more), the need
  for constraints on rules, the shortcomings of phrase-structure rules, Ross's constraints
  and syntactic islands. Sampled directly: the English particle-shift acceptability
  gradient (`threw out the notebook` → `threw the notebook Mary said John wanted back
  out`) as a performance/parsing-preference argument.
- **Ch. XII, Government and Binding / Principles-and-Parameters theory** — X-bar theory,
  bounding theory and subjacency, movement theory ("move α"), Case theory, theta theory,
  binding theory, the empty category principle, logical form, the three syntactic levels.
- **Ch. XIII, Later variants of generative grammar** — functional projections, binary
  branching and multiple-complement constructions, Minimalism (motivations, core
  principles, feature checking, copy-over-movement, "bare phrase structure"), the
  free-word-order problem, Optimality Theory, and an overall assessment of generative
  grammar.
- **Ch. XIV, Categorial grammar** (contributed by K.I. Kazenin) — first steps, Lambek
  calculus, modern categorial grammar.
- **Ch. XV, Functional syntactic typology** — A.E. Kibrik's explanatory typology; J.
  Hawkins's constituent-order theory (Early Immediate Constituent ratio; sampled directly:
  the worked EIC calculations for English particle-shift examples and the
  left-/right-branching-language puzzle via English/Japanese/Georgian long-constituent
  placement); a survey of other functionalist work; prospects for functionalism.
- **Ch. XVI, Syntax in the Meaning⟺Text model** — language modeling in the Mel'čuk/
  Zholkovsky MST tradition; overall model structure; the deep-syntactic level and
  component; the surface-syntactic component; an overall assessment of MST's syntactic
  component.

Each chapter ends with a "Рекомендуемая литература" (recommended reading) list. Back
matter: a substantial "Заключение" (conclusion), a "Литература" bibliography (~40 pages),
a subject index (~7 pages), and a Russian/English abbreviations glossary spanning both
Cyrillic grammatical-gloss codes (АБС, АКК, ГИнф, СИС, etc.) and standard generative-syntax
Latin abbreviations (X-bar, PRO/pro, D-structure/S-structure, EIC, and so on).

## Why this book, for this project

Per this project's own literature index, Testelets is used for two distinct purposes: (1)
by **RuTrans** (the RussianTranslation subproject) as a modern Russian general-syntax
textbook and source of standard Russian grammatical terminology when rendering Sanskrit
syntactic constructions into Russian; and (2) by **SaLex** as typological framing —
Part 2's chapter-by-chapter comparative tour of dependency grammar, constituency/generative
frameworks, categorial grammar, functional typology, and Meaning⟺Text theory gives a
neutral vocabulary for describing where a given syntactic analysis of Sanskrit sits relative
to competing 20th-century frameworks, without committing the project to any one school.
