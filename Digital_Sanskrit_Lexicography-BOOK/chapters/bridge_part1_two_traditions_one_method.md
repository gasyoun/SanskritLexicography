> Sets up Chapters 1–2.

A book that promises to grade the evidence behind lexicographic statements had better
begin by demonstrating two things: that the statements are *human* — made by editors with
stances, audiences, and inhibitions — and that they are nonetheless *measurable* without
flattening the humanity out of them. Part I does exactly that, once from each side.

Chapter 1 is the historical door, and it opens onto the least computational fact in the
book. When Böhtlingk glossed an obscene Sanskrit root in Latin rather than German, he was
not recording the language; he was deciding who might understand him. That decision — the
Latin discretion-screen, by which the Petersburg school and its heirs kept their
dictionaries simultaneously complete and "decent" — turns out to be recoverable, datable,
and countable: two thousand one hundred and four Latin-screened senses across eleven
dictionaries, a practice strongest in the Petersburg lexica, tightening between
Monier-Williams's two editions, fading by the late 1920s. The chapter serves the book
twice over. As history, it shows the European civilization making, reading, and censoring
its own record — the origin story of the entry-anatomy, sense-structure and citation
apparatus that Parts II–IV dissect. As method, it is the whole discipline in miniature:
the naive count ("count the Latin") is confounded, because Latin also feeds the learned
register of every European gloss language, and the honest measurement scores each gloss
against its own dictionary's register — the marked choice, not the raw string.

Chapter 2 then states, once and in full, the instrument the rest of the book assumes: a
catalog of estimators each defined with its floor and ceiling; a traceability discipline
under which every published number is walkable back to a dictionary line, every datum
carries an evidence grade, and no model inference reaches a published figure; a routing
discipline that keeps each claim falsifiable and inside its bounds; and the
bounded-witness rule (§6 there) that fixes, for the whole book, what the reference corpus
can and cannot testify to. The chapter's governing commitment — every claim a
`Claim → Evidence → Source` path, the machine proposing and a human ratifying — is the
evidence-graph thesis turned into working procedure.

The pairing is the point. A history chapter and a methods chapter open the book together
because the book's claim is that they are about the same thing: the dictionary as a
record of decisions, and the decisions as objects of measurement. Chapter 1 finds an
editor's stance toward his reader encoded in the language of a gloss and counts it under
controls; Chapter 2 generalises that instinct into a framework that will count sense
divisions, citations, cross-references and corrections the same way — as marked, graded,
source-bearing choices. The two-metalanguages motif that Chapter 1 opens — a European
tradition that speaks *about* Sanskrit in prestige screen-languages, an indigenous one
that speaks Sanskrit *in* Sanskrit and has no outsider to screen against — will return
at every structural level of the comparison; the measurement discipline that Chapter 2
fixes will decide, at every one of those levels, what the comparison is allowed to
conclude. Two traditions; one method. The remainder of the book is the method applied to
the traditions.
