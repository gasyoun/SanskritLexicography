An entry never stands alone. It points inward, at other entries — *cf.*, *see*, *Vgl.* —
and it points outward, at the texts on whose authority its senses rest. Those two pointer
systems are the dictionary's connective tissue: the mediostructure, in the field's
vocabulary, and the citation apparatus — and they are where this book's descent argument
and its evidence argument converge. A pointer is the most graph-like thing a dictionary
contains: an explicit, directed edge, asserted by an editor, inheritable by a successor,
and resolvable — or not — against a real text. Part IV measures both systems across the
family and ends by putting the book's central inheritance claim on trial.

The part works under a warrant the field itself has issued. For the cross-reference
apparatus, the discipline's own reference work concedes that a framework for systematic
study has yet to be developed (Hartmann and James 1998) — the rare case of a standard
reference naming the gap a chapter then fills. Chapter 9 builds such a framework and
learns its cautionary lesson: pointer graphs overlap for three reasons — shared
knowledge of the language, shared house conventions, and genuine inheritance — and only
calibration against a known-answer control (two editions of the same dictionary) plus
subtraction of convention-made hubs lets the third be read from the other two. So
calibrated, the family's most storied relationship — Monier-Williams against the
Petersburg lexicon — resolves into a shared scholarly core, decisively short of an
inherited network.

Chapter 10 turns to the outward pointers and finds the corpus split at its root: two
citation systems that do not translate into each other. The European dictionaries cite
through a tagged apparatus of source sigla — over 1.2 million citations, a majority
carrying locators that could, in principle, resolve to a passage. The great indigenous
lexica cite as densely, or more so, through the quotative *iti* — and score literally
zero on the European tag. One of the densest-citing dictionaries in the collection ties
for last under the European measure: the instrument-relativity rule of Parts II and III,
now at the level of evidence itself. No citation statistic in this family is
well-defined until the register is named — a finding with an exact comparative echo,
since the mature witness doctrines of the other lexicographic civilizations (the Arabic
*šawāhid* apparatus above all) show citation systems to be theorised, tradition-specific
constructions rather than a single universal practice (Baalbaki 2014).

Chapter 11 then asks what all this citing amounts to: which texts the tradition actually
rests on. Aggregated over the family's largest citation dataset — on the order of eight
hundred thousand tagged citations resolving to some nine hundred distinct works — the
citation-frequency graph is the dictionary family's collective bibliography, the
measured shape of its textual canon: which authorities carry the record, how steeply
their use falls away, and how differently the dictionaries weight them. At this scale
the statistical contract of Chapter 2 §6 binds in full — effect sizes and dispersion,
never bare significance — and every reading of the graph stays inside it.

Chapter 12 closes the part, and the descent argument, with the forensic case the whole
book has been building toward. "Monier-Williams copied Böhtlingk" can mean three things
— inventory, apparatus, errors — and only the third is stemmatic proof. Tested
separately, the signals return the book's most quotable verdict: the apparatus descends
— entry inventory, citations, even their order within the entry — while the errors do
not, and the prose is Monier-Williams's own. Heir of the scholarship, author of the
prose. The finding stands in a long comparative line — lexicographic traditions
everywhere copied, concealed, epitomised and accreted — but here, for the first time,
the inheritance is decomposed and measured rather than alleged.

Read in sequence, the four chapters carry the evidence graph through its mediostructural
layer: first the inward edges and what they can prove (Chapter 9), then the outward
edges and their two untranslatable registers (Chapter 10), then the outward edges
aggregated into a canon (Chapter 11), and finally the whole apparatus turned into
forensic evidence of descent (Chapter 12). What Part V adds is the last layer the graph
needs — the labels and corrections by which a dictionary keeps living after it is
printed.
