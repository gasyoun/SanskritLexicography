Before a dictionary says anything about any particular word, it has already made its
largest single assertion: the list itself. Which words exist for this dictionary, in what
order, reachable by what path — the macrostructure, in the canonical vocabulary of the
field (Hausmann and Wiegand 1989; Hartmann and James 1998) — is a claim about the
language as a whole, and the two civilizations of this book answer it with structures so
different that each is, to the other's instruments, nearly invisible. Part II measures
both answers on their own terms.

The European answer is the alphabetical headword inventory, and its first virtue is that
it can be audited as a *set*. Chapter 3 performs that audit across the collection: the
union headword index over the digitised family — 323,425 headwords with per-dictionary
provenance — read as a coverage geometry: how the dictionaries overlap and differ in
what they admit as a word; how the digitised record grew as the collection was rebuilt
and corrected across a decade; and, where the corpus can see at all, what fraction of
each dictionary's inventory carries attestation in the Digital Corpus of Sanskrit —
figures the book reads throughout as statements about the corpus's coverage, under the
bounded-witness rule of Chapter 2 §6, never as verdicts on the lexicographers. The
chapter is the book's documentary baseline: everything Parts III and IV measure lives
inside inventories whose size, growth and corpus-visibility Chapter 3 fixes.

Chapter 4 crosses to the indigenous answer, and the crossing requires abandoning the
question "what are this dictionary's entries?" altogether. The versified synonymic kośa
has no entries. It is *only* arrangement — a concept-ordered hierarchy of sections and
verses in which placing a word among its synonyms in the fire-verse of the heaven-book
*is* the assertion of its meaning, register and synonymy in one stroke. Chapter 4 models
that macrostructure as data: a cosmology encoded in section order (Brahmanical in one
kośa, Jain in its rival), a gender apparatus carried by verse-grammar, and the genre's
mass concentrated on the human world. It also establishes the caution the whole corpus
must respect: the same genre digitised under headword-primary and concept-primary
models yields entry counts that cannot be summed or compared — the digitisation
convention is a variable, not a constant.

Two threads tie the pair together, and both run onward through the book. First, the
contrast is not "order versus disorder" but *order versus order*: even the European
alphabet is not one thing — the lexicographic traditions this book keeps for comparison
sorted by first letters, by rhyme, by root-and-pattern permutation, and debated the
retrieval cost of each (Baalbaki 2014) — and the kośa's concept-order belongs to the
same broad onomasiological family as the European thesaurus tradition from the
*nomenclator* to Roget (Jackson 2002). What Part II measures is therefore two mature
solutions to the access problem, not a modern structure and an exotic one. Second, the
two lists are genealogically joined: the kośa synonym-sets flow, through the Fort
William pandits and Wilson, into the sense-columns of the very European dictionaries
whose inventory Chapter 3 audits — so the shape of the word-hoard measured here is also
the upstream end of the inheritance story that Parts III and IV will read at the level
of senses, cross-references and citations.
