> Sets up Chapters 13–14.

Everything measured so far could, in principle, have been measured on a dead corpus — a
family of dictionaries digitised once and left alone. The book's last two chapters
measure what only a *living* one can show. A dictionary lives in two directions: it
keeps speaking about the language, through the labels by which it sorts words into kinds
of Sanskrit; and it keeps being spoken back to, through the corrections by which its
readers repair it. Part V reads both directions as the evidence graph's final layers —
and in doing so closes the loop the Introduction opened, because the fact that this
family is still maintained is precisely what made it the book's testbed in the first
place.

Chapter 13 takes the labels. Digital practice has compressed Renou's history of Sanskrit
into five period-states and made them a timeline; the chapter restores what that
compression loses — Renou's own subsections are *registers*, not periods: the
commentarial idiom, the epigraphic witness, the narrative and dramatic and poetic
strata, each a kind of Sanskrit that cross-cuts the calendar. Operationalised as a
second, orthogonal, provenance-graded annotation axis, the register turns out to do
epistemic work no period tag can: the épigraphique stratum — inscriptional Sanskrit,
absent from a literary corpus *by construction* — is exactly where corpus attestation
fails, and the register label is what makes that failure a measured boundary instead of
an invisible hole. It is the bounded-witness rule of Chapter 2 §6 given its worked
example: the label maps the edge of what the corpus can see. Register marking itself has
run through every tradition this book has touched — the Roman scholiast's three-register
decorum whose obscene extreme opened Chapter 1, the Greek Atticists' normative
admissions, the modern diasystematic label — and the chapter's contribution is to make
the axis explicit, multi-valued and graded rather than implicit in an editor's habits.

Chapter 14 takes the corrections, and with them the book arrives at the layer its thesis
named first: review provenance. Twelve years of collaborative maintenance — 52,498
dated, attributed correction events across forty-three dictionaries — are assembled into
a typed corpus and read as an error typology with a deliberately double-edged title:
surface, not substance. The corrections cluster exactly where meaning lives, in
definitions and headwords, yet are overwhelmingly small surface repairs; they form a
per-dictionary fingerprint, not a uniform noise floor; and the fingerprint shifts with
the community's curatorial priorities. The chapter's reading rule is the part's deepest
lesson: a correction record measures where curators chose to look, not how wrong a
dictionary is — corrected is not the same as bad, and a heavily corrected dictionary may
simply be a well-loved one. Attention, not error rate. That the maintenance history
arrives already speaking the book's evidence vocabulary — each event labelled with the
same grades the graph assigns to every other statement — is the thesis's most direct
instantiation: the dictionary's upkeep is itself first-class, gradeable, corpus-scale
data.

Together the two chapters complete the evidence graph's annotation stack — source and
convention (Parts II–III), citation and descent (Part IV), and now register and review —
which is to say they finish the book's constructive work. What remains is the argument
the Conclusion makes: that the completed stack is not a Sanskrit curiosity but a general
model, and that what the two civilizations teach, measured together, is addressed to
lexicography at large.
