## 1. Widening the lens

At this point the book crosses into the entry — the microstructure, where a dictionary
does its closest work — and before it does, the comparison that has so far run between
two civilizations should be placed among the others. Sanskrit is not the only language
over which a complete, self-aware lexicographic civilization grew. Arabic built one of
the great ones: a continuous, doctrinally explicit tradition from the eighth century to
the eighteenth, whose historian can document its macrostructural typologies, its
evidentiary rules and its borrowing practices across a millennium (Baalbaki 2014). Latin
built another, from the glossae and Verrius Flaccus through Nonius to the humanist
dictionaries, studied essay by essay in the modern literature (Ferri 2019, in Maltby et
al.); Greek scholarship built the lexica and scholia whose apparatus Dickey (2007)
surveys. Setting the Sanskrit case beside these is not comparative ornament. It converts
claims of the form "this is unusual" into claims of the form "this is a known solution,
here carried further" — and it isolates, precisely, the few places where the Sanskrit
evidence is genuinely without parallel.

Three axes organise the comparison, and the book has met or will meet all three.

**Order.** Every lexicographic civilization faced the same design fork: a word-list is
either a retrieval instrument or a semantic map, and each choice taxes the other. The
Arabic tradition theorised the fork explicitly — semasiological *muǧannas* lexica
against onomasiological *mubawwab* works — and ran, in parallel, three distinct formal
orders: al-Khalīl's phonetic-permutative arrangement, plain alphabetical order, and the
rhyme order that organised the tradition's largest dictionaries (Baalbaki 2014). Greek
practice sorted by first letter or first syllable, rarely by full alphabetization
(Dickey 2007). Part II has just shown the Sanskrit resolution: the kośa takes the
semantic-map branch to its limit — a dictionary that is *only* arrangement — while the
European dictionaries of the family take the retrieval branch. The fork is
civilizational, the trade-off universal; what varies is how far each side is pushed.

**Witness.** Who counts as evidence for a word, and until when? Here the Arabic
tradition out-formalises everyone: the *šawāhid* apparatus of probative quotations came
with an explicit doctrine of *ʿuṣūr al-iḥtiǧāǧ* — epochs of reliable usage after which
speakers no longer count — and with informant-screening practices for admitting Bedouin
testimony (Baalbaki 2014). The Greek Atticists marked usage normatively, admitting and
proscribing by register (Dickey 2007); Nonius Marcellus built his Latin dictionary on a
closed canon of some forty *auctores*, cited in an order so fixed it became a
reconstruction tool (Ferri 2019, Gatti). Part IV will measure the Sanskrit counterparts:
the European tagged citation apparatus against the indigenous *iti*-quotation — two
witness systems in one corpus — and the citation-frequency graph of what the tradition
actually cites. The comparative point, worth carrying in: an explicit theory of *which
attestations confer authority* is not a modern methodological luxury but a recurring
feature of mature lexicographic civilizations. The evidence-grading this book proposes
has, in that sense, pre-modern ancestors.

**Copying.** Inheritance is the genre's normal condition everywhere. The Arabic
tradition documents a millennium of acknowledged and concealed borrowing — Ibn Manẓūr
absorbing a predecessor's dictionary without naming him (Baalbaki 2014); the Latin line
runs by epitome and accretion, Verrius shrinking through Festus to Paul the Deacon,
Calepino swelling into Estienne (Ferri 2019, Lhommé and Furno); and the Homeric scholia
even stacked four commentators with a recorded source-precedence ordering — a
pre-digital layered evidence graph (Dickey 2007). Chapters 9 and 12 will give the
Sanskrit family's version a forensic treatment the comparanda have never received:
descent measured graph-wide against positive controls, and the Monier-Williams–
Petersburg inheritance decomposed into inventory, apparatus and error, each tested
separately. The civilizations supply the phenomenon; the digital corpus supplies, for
the first time, the measurement.

One asymmetry should be stated as honestly as the parallels. The comparison runs on
secondary literature, not on parallel measurement — the Arabic, Latin and Greek corpora
have no equivalent of the CDSL to be measured against — so it frames the Sanskrit
findings; it does not replicate them. And one Sanskrit-European finding has no
counterpart on any of the three axes or anywhere else in the comparanda: neither the
Arabic correctness-screen, which polices purity rather than modesty, nor Latin
scholiastic decorum produced a *sexual*-taboo screen of the kind Chapter 1 measured.
That practice remains, on present evidence, Europe-specific and high-Victorian — the
comparison sharpens its originality rather than dissolving it.

## 2. Inside the entry

With the lens widened, the four chapters of this part enter the entry from its two
civilizational sides.

Chapter 5 anatomises the European entry at full depth, on the family's largest member.
Rather than importing a European framework wholesale, it builds a grounded one from
Monier-Williams's 286,561 entries — block, slot, profile, hedge, infrastructure — and
only then triangulates against the imported traditions, which corroborate the reading
without having predicted its central finding: a lean block-kernel maintained across a
quarter-million entries, with enrichment driven by word-type. The entry emerges as an
*economy* — the print economics of a single volume disciplining what any entry may
carry — and its most distinctive single feature is a hedge: the lexicographers-only
attestation marker by which the dictionary flags a word it records but cannot show in
use. A dictionary marking the boundary of its own evidence, in 1899, is the
evidence-graph thesis avant la lettre, and the entry's designed-ness is what Apresjan's
notion of the lexicographic portrait — the entry as a systematic, rule-shaped
description — named for the modern era (Apresjan 2000).

Chapter 6 takes up the sense, the entry's most contested component, under the warning
the theoretical literature has posted for decades: senses may be artifacts of the
dictionary rather than units of the language (Lew 2013). The chapter measures what the
dictionaries assert anyway, in two halves: inheritance — where sense granularity proves
to be a trait of lexicographic *school*, not a function of time, and descendants
condense rather than inflate — and order, where the philological dictionaries lead with
the generative sense (the *genetic* principle Apresjan formalised) while the
pedagogical ones lead by salience. Both halves test a named theory against a documented
family at corpus scale.

Chapters 7 and 8 then perform this part's crossing. The indigenous entry, measured by
European detectors, scores near zero — no part-of-speech tags, no citation elements, no
sense markers — and the two chapters show the zero to be an artifact of the instrument,
exactly as Part II's macrostructural crossing predicted. Chapter 7 recovers the
verbal-root microstructure: a complete conjugational grammar — class, voice,
transitivity — encoded in the kośa's own conventions, decoded at scale, and validated by
the agreement of five independently compiled lexica. The organising principle has a
precise Arabic analogue: as the Arabic lexicon organises by root-and-pattern (*jaḏr* and
*wazn*), the indigenous Sanskrit lexicon organises around the *dhātu*, and in both
civilizations the entry presupposes the grammar — the integrated grammar–dictionary
description Apresjan's school argued for in the twentieth century, practiced in Sanskrit
for centuries (Apresjan 2000; Baalbaki 2014). Chapter 8 descends to the deepest shared
layer, derivation: the Pāṇinian analysis of a headword into root, affix and grammatical
role, recorded across ten lexica in three scholarly lineages. Its Arabic mirror is
exact — the oldest complete Arabic dictionary is organised on derivational extraction
(*al-ištiqāq*) and stands beside Sībawayhi's grammar as its twin (Baalbaki 2014) — while
its Latin near-miss is instructive: classical etymologizing was associative and
rhetorical, not rule-governed, and the chapter is careful not to flatter it into a
*vyutpatti* (Ferri 2019, Pieroni). Measured under the notation, the indigenous lexica
agree on the derivational affix in over ninety percent of double-covered cases: beneath
the least standardised-looking half of the corpus lies its most consistent layer.

That inversion — the "untagged" tradition proving the most internally consistent — is
the part's destination, and the reason its bridge went through the other civilizations:
against the comparative background, the indigenous Sanskrit lexicon stops looking like
a poor relation of the European dictionary and takes its place among the great
grammar-integrated lexicographic systems, with the European philological apparatus as
the *other* mature technology in the room. The entry, on both sides, is design; the
next four chapters measure it.
